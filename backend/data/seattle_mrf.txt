SEATTLE MRF SPECIFICATIONS
Facility: Recology King County Materials Recovery Facility (MRF), SODO
Curbside Hauler: Recology King County (formerly CleanScapes)
City: Seattle, Washington
Program Type: Single-stream curbside recycling
Last Updated: 2024

NOTE ON SOURCING: This document was compiled from Recology King County's official
Seattle recycling FAQ, Seattle Public Utilities (SPU) guidance, a January 2024 Seattle
Times editorial board tour of the Recology King County MRF, and UW Sustainability
recycling documentation. Where a claim could not be verified against these official
sources, it is flagged [UNVERIFIED].

-------------------------------------------------------------------
PLASTICS
-------------------------------------------------------------------

SEATTLE'S APPROACH: Ignore the resin code number. SPU and Recology explicitly advise
residents NOT to sort by the numbered triangle symbol. Instead, focus on the physical
form of the item: rigid vs. flexible.

ACCEPTED — rigid plastic items:
  - Bottles (all colors, all resin codes): water bottles, soda bottles, shampoo,
    detergent, condiment bottles, etc.
  - Jugs (all colors): milk jugs, juice jugs, laundry detergent jugs.
  - Food containers and trays.
  - Dairy tubs (yogurt, sour cream, cottage cheese containers).
  - Plastic cups (cold and hot drink cups — rigid only).
  - Clear plastic clamshell containers (berry containers, salad containers, etc.).
    Note: clamshells are technically harder to sort than bottles. Keep them clean
    and empty to avoid contamination issues.
  - Lids — accepted if 3 inches (7.6 cm) or wider. Smaller lids fall through
    conveyor belt screens.
  - Plastic plant pots.
  - Plastic buckets.
  - Pill bottles (not prescription vials).
  - PVC pipe (white only).
  - Household rigid plastic items: furniture, laundry baskets, storage bins, etc.
  - All containers must be empty and rinsed.

NOT ACCEPTED — regardless of resin code:
  - Black plastic of any kind. NIR (Near-Infrared) optical sorters cannot detect
    carbon-black pigmented polymers, which absorb infrared light. Black takeout
    boxes, black plastic lids, black trays — all go to trash.
  - Plastic bags, films, and flexible/stretchy plastic of any kind. These wrap
    around conveyor belts and augers, triggering emergency shutdowns that can
    halt sorting lines for up to an hour. Return to store drop-off locations
    (most major grocery stores accept them). This has been in effect since
    January 2020.
    Examples: grocery bags, bread bags, Ziploc bags, cling wrap, bubble wrap,
    dry cleaning bags, plastic wrap on paper towels/toilet paper.
  - Styrofoam / EPS foam of any kind: blocks, packing peanuts, foam cups,
    foam trays. Note: Washington State banned EPS foam food service products
    effective 2024.
  - Compostable plastics (PLA, corn-based plastic). These look like regular
    plastic but cannot be recycled and do not break down at a MRF. Place in
    the food/yard (green) bin if labeled BPI Certified Compostable, or trash.

MINIMUM ITEM SIZE: Larger than a standard credit card (approx. 3.37 x 2.125 inches).
Smaller items fall through conveyor belt screens into the "fines" stream and are
landfilled. [UNVERIFIED — specific size threshold not confirmed in official SPU sources;
however, the general principle of minimum size is consistent with MRF operations.]

PAPER AND CARDBOARD
-------------------------------------------------------------------

ACCEPTED:
  - Corrugated cardboard. Must be flattened and dry. Break down all boxes.
  - Newspaper, office paper, magazines, catalogs, junk mail.
  - Envelopes, including windowed envelopes (the plastic window can remain).
  - Paper bags.
  - Paper cups.
  - Paperboard: cereal boxes, cracker boxes, tissue boxes. Remove plastic liners.
  - Cartons: milk cartons, juice boxes, Tetra Paks, aseptic containers, soy milk
    cartons.
  - Phone books and paperback books.
  - Non-metallic wrapping paper.
  - Frozen food boxes.

NOT ACCEPTED:
  - Wet or heavily soiled paper. Wet paper loses fiber integrity and contaminates
    dry paper bales.
  - Waxed cardboard (e.g., produce boxes from grocery stores). Wax coating
    prevents fiber separation during repulping.
  - Receipts, thermal paper, fax paper. Thermal coating contaminates paper bales.
  - Shredded paper — NOT accepted loose. It falls through conveyor screens and
    jams equipment. Place shredded paper in a sealed paper bag or small cardboard
    box if putting in recycling.

PIZZA BOXES: If the box is lightly greasy (bottom of box slightly stained), it is
generally accepted. If heavily soaked through with grease, place in the trash or
tear off the clean lid and recycle that portion. Grease can contaminate paper fiber
bales and cause commodity buyers to reject entire bales.
Note: The "25% grease rule" cited in some sources has not been confirmed in official
SPU or Recology guidance. [UNVERIFIED — treat as a useful rule of thumb only.]

-------------------------------------------------------------------
GLASS
-------------------------------------------------------------------

ACCEPTED:
  - Glass bottles and jars: clear, brown, and green.
  - Must be empty and rinsed.

NOT ACCEPTED:
  - Broken glass. Shards embed in paper fiber bales and contaminate them, and
    pose laceration hazards to MRF workers. If glass breaks in your bin,
    double-bag it in plastic and place in trash.
  - Window glass and mirrors.
  - Light bulbs (incandescent and LED go to trash; fluorescent/CFL bulbs go to
    LightRecycle.org drop-off locations — free).
  - Ceramics, dishes, and Pyrex / heat-resistant glass. These have different
    chemical compositions than container glass and contaminate glass cullet.

-------------------------------------------------------------------
METALS
-------------------------------------------------------------------

ACCEPTED:
  - Aluminum cans. Must be empty and rinsed.
  - Steel and tin cans. Must be empty and rinsed.
  - Aerosol cans, if completely empty (shake to verify no liquid remains).
  - Aluminum foil and foil trays, if clean (free of food residue). Crumple
    multiple pieces together into a ball at least 2 inches in diameter so
    individual small pieces don't fall through conveyor screens.
  - Metal lids and caps: place inside a metal can and crimp the top closed so
    they don't fall through belt gaps.
  - Ferrous scrap metal and other scrap metals smaller than 2' x 2' x 2'.

NOT ACCEPTED curbside:
  - Large scrap metal, pots, pans, and appliances. Take to a scrap metal
    dealer or SPU Transfer Station.

-------------------------------------------------------------------
TANGLERS (CRITICAL CONTAMINATION CATEGORY)
-------------------------------------------------------------------

Tanglers are among the top causes of MRF equipment shutdowns in Seattle.
A single tangler can stop a sorting line for 30–60 minutes and can damage
expensive machinery. NEVER put any of the following in the recycling bin:

  - Garden hoses (any length or material) — one of the most damaging tanglers
  - Extension cords and electrical cables
  - Christmas lights and string lights
  - Chains, ropes, and twine
  - Clothing, textiles, and fabric of any kind
  - Bed sheets, towels, and linens
  - Plastic bags and plastic film (see Plastics section)
  - Wire and metal cables
  - Rubber bands in large quantities

Source: Confirmed by 2024 Seattle Times editorial board tour of Recology's
King County MRF, which described tanglers as capable of sabotaging "the vital
and very expensive" sorting equipment.

-------------------------------------------------------------------
ORGANICS (SEPARATE STREAM — GREEN BIN)
-------------------------------------------------------------------

Seattle operates a separate food and yard waste composting program through
Cedar Grove Composting.

ACCEPTED in the green food/yard waste bin:
  - All food scraps: fruit, vegetables, meat, fish, dairy, bread, grains,
    coffee grounds, eggshells.
  - Food-soiled paper: paper napkins, paper towels, paper plates, uncoated
    paper bags.
  - Compostable serviceware labeled "BPI Certified Compostable."
  - Yard waste: grass clippings, leaves, branches, plants, untreated wood.

IMPORTANT: Do NOT put compostables in the recycling bin. They contaminate
recycling bales and do not break down at a MRF. This includes compostable
plastics (PLA).

-------------------------------------------------------------------
ITEMS REQUIRING SPECIAL DISPOSAL
-------------------------------------------------------------------

  - Electronics (e-waste): Take to e-cycle drop-off. See ecyclewa.org — TVs,
    computers, monitors accepted free.
  - Batteries: Take to hazwastehelp.org drop-off locations or SPU Transfer
    Stations (free). NEVER put lithium batteries in trash or recycling — they
    are a fire hazard in compactor trucks and have caused truck fires.
  - Medications: Use King County medication drop-off boxes.
    See medtakebackwashington.org (free).
  - Motor oil: Take to auto parts stores (AutoZone, O'Reilly accept used motor
    oil). Or set next to recycling cart in a labeled 1-gallon container (free,
    up to 2 gallons per collection day).
  - Paint: Use PaintCare drop-off locations at paintcare.org/drop-off-sites (free).
  - Syringes / sharps: Place in a hard plastic bottle or container. See
    seattle.gov/utilities/sharps (free).
  - Fluorescent light bulbs / CFLs: Take to lightrecycle.org drop-off (free).
  - Clothes and textiles: Donate to thrift stores or use drop boxes.
    See kingcounty.gov/threadcycle for recycling options.
  - Plastic clamshells (if you want extra assurance): Ridwell offers a paid
    subscription pickup service for clamshells in parts of Seattle if you want
    to keep them out of the general single-stream bin.
  - Plastic film / bags: Return to grocery store drop-off bins.
    See nextrex.com for locations.

COMMON MISTAKES AND THEIR CONSEQUENCES
-------------------------------------------------------------------

Putting a black plastic container in recycling:
  NIR optical sorter cannot detect it. It passes through unsorted, is likely
  landfilled anyway, and may contaminate nearby items on the belt.

Putting a plastic bag in recycling:
  Wraps around rotating auger shaft, triggers emergency stop, requires manual
  removal, shuts down line for up to an hour. Significant safety risk and
  operational cost.

Leaving food residue in containers:
  Contaminates paper fiber bales, causes mold, results in rejection of entire
  bales by commodity buyers.

Recycling broken glass:
  Embeds in paper bales, causes laceration injuries to MRF workers, causes
  bale rejection.

Putting a garden hose in recycling:
  Wraps around belt rollers. One of the most damaging tangler types. Can
  damage expensive equipment and shut down operations for extended periods.

Putting a lithium battery in the recycling or trash:
  Puncture risk during compaction causes thermal runaway fires. Compactor
  truck fires are a documented and serious consequence.

"Wish-cycling" (putting non-recyclables in hoping they'll be recycled):
  Contaminates good recyclable material, decreases bale value, and results
  in more material going to landfill — not less.

QUICK REFERENCE: SHAPE RULE FOR PLASTICS
-------------------------------------------------------------------

  RECYCLE if it is:  Rigid + bottle/jug/tub/cup/clamshell/container shape
  TRASH if it is:    Flexible, stretchy, filmy, or foamy
  TRASH if it is:    Black plastic (any shape)
  COMPOST if it is:  Labeled BPI Certified Compostable
